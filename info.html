The Execution Process

The Gibbet‚Äôs mechanism was straightforward:

  • A wooden platform held the condemned prisoner.
  • A block of wood with an embedded iron axe blade was hoisted to the top.
  • The block was released by cutting a rope or pulling a pin, dropping the blade to deliver a swift beheading.
  • The design ensured a quick execution, minimizing suffering.

Executions were public events, often drawing large crowds.

The efficiency and spectacle of the Gibbet made it both feared and notorious across the region.

The Power of justice and the LAW and Blade

The gibbet blade, or axe, weighed 7lb 12oz [3.2Kg] and was attached to a block of wood suspended by a piece of rope from the top of the gibbet. This would deliver a crushing blow to the neck. The rope would be attached to a pin at the side of the gibbet platform; it is said that in the case of a cloth theft the rope would be passed among the crowd, while in the case of stock theft, the rope would be attached to the stolen animal, whatever it was. Other methods was the use of a horse - once the horse was instructed to move the blade fell, cutting swiftly through the neck.

Public outrage: opposition of gibbet

It is impossible to determine with any accuracy how many individuals were executed using the Halifax Gibbet. By 1650, public opinion deemed beheading an excessively severe punishment for petty theft. Consequently, Oliver Cromwell, Lord Protector of the Commonwealth of England, forbade the use of the gibbet, and the structure was subsequently dismantled.

Key Historical Events

The first recorded execution occurred in 1286, with John Dalton being beheaded.

16th Century: Introduction of the Halifax Gibbet

A guillotine-like device, known as the Halifax Gibbet, was constructed to carry out executions. The device featured a heavy wooden block with an axe head, sliding between two upright posts, and was operated by releasing a pin or cutting a rope.

1541‚Äì1650: Active Use of the Gibbet

Between 1541 and 1650, 53 individuals were executed using the Halifax Gibbet. Executions typically took place on market days, serving as a public deterrent.

1650: Last Recorded Execution

The final executions occurred in April 1650, when Anthony Mitchell and John Wilkinson were beheaded for stealing cloth and horses. Following public outcry over the severity of the punishment for petty theft, Oliver Cromwell prohibited the use of the gibbet.

1840: Rediscovery of the Gibbet Platform

The original stone platform supporting the gibbet was rediscovered, leading to preservation efforts.

1970s: Reconstruction of the Gibbet

A replica of the Halifax Gibbet was constructed near the original site, serving as a historical monument and educational exhibit.
